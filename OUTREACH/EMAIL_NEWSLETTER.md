## EMAIL 3 — WEEK 2 DRIP (AI TOOL DEEP DIVE)

Subject line: Deep dive: why deposition AI is the highest-ROI workflow in litigation right now

Preheader: A full breakdown of one tool category, why it matters, and how to evaluate it in under an hour.

---

Two weeks in. Thank you for still being here.

Today's email is a deep dive on one specific category of legal AI, because deep dives are what our readers tell us they value most. I will go into enough detail that you can walk into a partner meeting tomorrow and speak credibly about this category, and I will link to the full directory pages where you can explore specific tools when you are ready.

The category: deposition analysis AI.

Why it matters more than you might think.

Deposition prep and review is one of the most time-intensive, highest-leverage workflows in any litigation practice. A single deposition transcript can run 300 pages. A typical matter has half a dozen or more. Preparing cross-examination outlines, spotting contradictions across witness testimony, and tracking every reference to a key exhibit is painstaking work that junior associates and paralegals have traditionally absorbed.

Deposition AI collapses that effort. The current generation of tools ingests raw transcripts, produces issue-coded summaries, generates first-draft cross-examination outlines, flags contradictions within and across witness testimony, and tracks exhibit references throughout the record. The best tools do all of this in minutes and produce output that is useful enough to hand to a senior partner without embarrassment.

The ROI is remarkable for three reasons. First, the time savings are measured in hours per witness, not minutes. Second, the work is bounded and repeatable, which means the quality is consistent. Third, the risk is contained. A deposition summary that is 95 percent accurate is still enormously useful because the attorney is always going to verify the key points. You are not relying on the AI for judgment, only for first-pass compression.

How to evaluate a deposition AI tool in under an hour.

Start with one transcript you know well. Not a representative sample, not a standardized benchmark, an actual transcript from a recent matter where you personally know what the witness said and what the key contradictions were. Run it through any tool you are considering and ask yourself two questions. Did the tool find everything you would have found. Did it find anything you missed.

If the answers are yes and yes, you have a viable tool. If either answer is no, dig in. Was it a prompt issue, a model limitation, or a fundamental architectural problem. The last one is disqualifying.

Then test on a transcript you do not know well. This is where the real evaluation happens. How much do you trust the output when you cannot verify every claim from memory. If you find yourself wanting to re-read the transcript anyway, the tool is not adding enough value.
