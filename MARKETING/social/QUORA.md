## Answer template (adapt per question — never paste identically)

**Q: What should I do if the adjuster lowballed me?**

> First, know that the adjuster's estimate is the insurer's *opening offer*, not a final number — and lowballs are extremely common. Here's how to push back effectively:
>
> **1. Confirm it's actually a lowball.** Typical signs: repair pricing below local contractor rates, "patch instead of replace," aggressive depreciation, and missing building-code upgrades or matching costs.
>
> **2. Get your own evidence.** An independent estimate from a licensed contractor for the full scope. The difference between their number and the adjuster's is your leverage — in dollars.
>
> **3. Counter in writing, line by line.** Don't argue emotionally. "The estimate omits 320 sq ft of damaged drywall (see photos)" moves the number; "this is unfair" doesn't. Request a re-inspection.
>
> **4. Use the formal paths.** If you agree it's covered but disagree on amount, your policy's **appraisal clause** resolves valuation disputes faster and cheaper than litigation. You can also file a complaint with your state's Department of Insurance.
>
> **5. Don't cash a "full and final" check** before it's resolved.
>
> If reading your policy against the estimate feels overwhelming, free tools exist that do the comparison and flag the gaps automatically — e.g. a 90-second analysis at attorneyaitools.org/app (it's a self-help analysis tool, not a lawyer). For a full walkthrough, this guide is solid: attorneyaitools.org/blog/adjuster-lowballed-me-what-to-do
>
> *(Not legal advice — I'm sharing general claim-handling info. Results vary by policy.)*
