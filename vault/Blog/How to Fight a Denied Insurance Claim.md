# How to Fight a Denied Insurance Claim

A denial letter feels final. It isn't. Insurers deny or underpay a large share of valid claims, and a documented, persistent challenge frequently reverses the outcome — often recovering thousands of dollars more than the first offer. Here is exactly how to fight back.

## Step 1: Read the denial letter like a contract

Every denial cites a reason. Find the exact one. Common categories:

- **Excluded peril** — the insurer says the cause of loss isn't covered (e.g. flood under a homeowners policy).
- **"Wear and tear" / maintenance** — they're calling the damage gradual rather than sudden.
- **Insufficient documentation** — they say you didn't prove the loss.
- **Missed deadline** — late notice or a "suit limitation" clause.
- **Below deductible** — they scoped the damage low enough to fall under your deductible.

Match that reason against your actual policy language. Denials are often based on a narrow reading that the right evidence can defeat.

## Step 2: Get your own evidence

The adjuster's file is the insurer's version of events. Build yours:

- Photos and video of all damage, dated.
- An **independent repair estimate** from a licensed contractor. This is your single strongest tool — a contractor's scope routinely exceeds the adjuster's.
- Receipts, the original policy declarations page, and a written timeline of the loss.

## Step 3: Request a re-inspection in writing

Ask, in writing, for a re-inspection and a line-item explanation of the scope. Insurers frequently revise their estimate when shown specific, documented gaps — damaged square footage they missed, code-required upgrades, matching materials.

## Step 4: Write an itemized rebuttal

Don't argue emotionally. For each disputed item, state: what the policy covers, what your evidence shows, and what the correct payment should be. Attach the contractor estimate and photos. A clear, organized rebuttal signals you're prepared to escalate.

## Step 5: Use the formal escalation paths

If the rebuttal stalls:

- **Appraisal clause.** Most property policies include one — a process for resolving valuation disputes using independent appraisers and an umpire. It's faster and cheaper than litigation.
- **Department of Insurance complaint.** Every state has one (find yours via the [NAIC directory](https://content.naic.org/state-insurance-departments)). A regulator complaint often gets a stalled claim moving.
- **Bad-faith review.** If the insurer denied without a reasonable basis, you may have a bad-faith claim — worth a lawyer's review.

## Step 6: Mind the deadlines

Most policies require *prompt notice* of loss and set a contractual time limit to file suit (often one to two years). Check your policy's suit-limitation clause and confirm specifics with your state's Department of Insurance. Missing these can end an otherwise strong claim.
