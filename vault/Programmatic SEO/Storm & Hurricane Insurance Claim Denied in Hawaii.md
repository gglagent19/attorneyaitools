## Why Storm & Hurricane Insurance Claims Get Denied in Hawaii

When a storm and hurricane claim is underpaid in Hawaii, it usually traces back to one of these:

- Wind damage was reclassified as flood damage to push it outside the homeowners policy
- A separate (higher) hurricane or wind/hail deductible was applied
- The insurer argued damage pre-dated the named storm
- The scope omitted interior water intrusion that followed roof or window failure

In Hawaii, where hurricanes, flooding, and volcanic activity drive a large share of property losses, storm and hurricane claims are especially prone to causation disputes — insurers may attribute the damage to an excluded cause to reduce or deny payment.

## What a Storm & Hurricane Insurance Lowball Looks Like in Hawaii

A lowball on a storm and hurricane claim in Hawaii usually means splitting wind vs. flood causation to minimize payout and applying the highest available deductible. The number can look official — letterhead, line items — but the scope behind it is often incomplete. Comparing the adjuster's storm and hurricane estimate line-by-line against real Hawaii repair costs is where most underpayments surface.

## How to dispute a storm and hurricane claim in Hawaii

1. **Decode the denial.** Find the specific exclusion or scope item the adjuster cited on your Hawaii claim.
2. **Document everything** in Hawaii — dated photos, video, receipts, and a written timeline of the loss.
3. **Get an independent estimate** from a licensed Hawaii contractor — the gap between their scope and the adjuster's is your leverage.
4. **Request a re-inspection in writing** and submit an itemized rebuttal that ties each disputed item to your policy and your evidence.
5. **Take it higher** — file with the Hawaii Department of Insurance ([find it here](https://content.naic.org/state-insurance-departments)), or invoke your policy's appraisal provision for amount disputes.

> **Watch the clock.** Your Hawaii policy almost certainly has a "suit limitation" clause and a prompt-notice requirement. Verify both against your own contract and the Hawaii Department of Insurance before they cost you the claim.
