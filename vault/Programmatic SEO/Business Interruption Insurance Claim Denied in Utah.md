## Why Business Interruption Insurance Claims Get Denied in Utah

Most business interruption disputes in Utah come down to a handful of recurring tactics:

- The period of restoration was cut short, ending lost-income payments early
- Extra expense and payroll continuation were excluded
- The lost-income calculation used conservative revenue assumptions
- A covered physical-loss trigger was disputed

In Utah, where wildfires and winter storms drive a large share of property losses, business interruption claims are especially prone to causation disputes — insurers may attribute the damage to an excluded cause to reduce or deny payment.

## How to dispute a business interruption claim in Utah

1. **Read the denial or estimate closely.** Pin down the exact policy provision your insurer leaned on for this business interruption claim.
2. **Document everything** in Utah — dated photos, video, receipts, and a written timeline of the loss.
3. **Commission your own Utah contractor estimate.** Where it exceeds the insurer's figure is exactly what you negotiate back.
4. **Request a re-inspection in writing** and submit an itemized rebuttal that ties each disputed item to your policy and your evidence.
5. **Use the formal channels:** a complaint to the Utah Department of Insurance ([NAIC](https://content.naic.org/state-insurance-departments)) and, for valuation-only disputes, the appraisal clause.

> **Time limits matter here.** Utah policies typically cap how long you have to act. Check your policy's deadline clause and the Utah Department of Insurance so a technicality never closes your file.

Yes. A denial or low offer on a business interruption claim in Utah is the start of a negotiation, not the end. You can request a re-inspection, submit an itemized rebuttal, invoke your policy's appraisal clause, and escalate to the Utah Department of Insurance.

**Is the insurer's first offer final?**
