## Why Business Interruption Insurance Claims Get Denied in Montana

Most business interruption disputes in Montana come down to a handful of recurring tactics:

- The period of restoration was cut short, ending lost-income payments early
- Extra expense and payroll continuation were excluded
- The lost-income calculation used conservative revenue assumptions
- A covered physical-loss trigger was disputed

In Montana, where wildfires, hail, and winter storms drive a large share of property losses, business interruption claims are especially prone to causation disputes — insurers may attribute the damage to an excluded cause to reduce or deny payment.

## What a Business Interruption Insurance Lowball Looks Like in Montana

In Montana, an underpaid business interruption offer typically comes from understating projected revenue, shortening the restoration period, and excluding continuing payroll and extra expenses. The number can look official — letterhead, line items — but the scope behind it is often incomplete. Comparing the adjuster's business interruption estimate line-by-line against real Montana repair costs is where most underpayments surface.

## How to dispute a business interruption claim in Montana

1. **Read the denial or estimate closely.** Pin down the exact policy provision your insurer leaned on for this business interruption claim.
2. **Document everything** in Montana — dated photos, video, receipts, and a written timeline of the loss.
3. **Commission your own Montana contractor estimate.** Where it exceeds the insurer's figure is exactly what you negotiate back.
4. **Request a re-inspection in writing** and submit an itemized rebuttal that ties each disputed item to your policy and your evidence.
5. **Use the formal channels:** a complaint to the Montana Department of Insurance ([NAIC](https://content.naic.org/state-insurance-departments)) and, for valuation-only disputes, the appraisal clause.

> **Time limits matter here.** Montana policies typically cap how long you have to act. Check your policy's deadline clause and the Montana Department of Insurance so a technicality never closes your file.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I dispute a business interruption claim in Montana?**

Yes. A denial or low offer on a business interruption claim in Montana is the start of a negotiation, not the end. You can request a re-inspection, submit an itemized rebuttal, invoke your policy's appraisal clause, and escalate to the Montana Department of Insurance.

**Do I need a lawyer to fight a business interruption claim in Montana?**

Not always. Many Montana valuation disputes are resolved with a documented rebuttal or the appraisal process. A lawyer makes sense for outright coverage denials or bad-faith conduct. You can also run a free analysis first to see how large your gap is.

**How long do I have to appeal in Montana?**

Montana policies usually set a contractual deadline to file suit — commonly one to two years from the loss — plus a prompt-notice requirement. Check your policy's "suit limitation" clause and confirm with the Montana Department of Insurance.
